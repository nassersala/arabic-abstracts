## Paper Structure & Content

### Section 1: Introduction
- Establishes need for digital accessibility expertise
- Introduces Digital Accessibility Literacy (DAL) concept
- Highlights gap in pedagogical culture for accessibility education

### Section 2: Teaching Accessibility in Various Disciplines

**2.1 Computer Science:**
- Only 15% of CS faculty teach accessibility [5]
- 52% say it's not in curriculum [13]
- "Good practice problem" - everyone copies each other [7]
- Proposal: integrate as cross-cutting theme

**2.2 Pedagogy:**
- TPACK model for teacher competencies
- DigCompEdu framework (6 areas, 22 competencies)
- UNESCO ICT Competency Framework
- Need to expand frameworks for accessibility

### Section 3: Literacy Concepts
- Literacy = encoding + decoding
- Paul Gilster's Digital Literacy (1997) [3]
- Critical thinking about technology's impact
- Data literacy example structure
- Application to accessibility domain

### Section 4: DAL Framework (CORE CONTRIBUTION)

**Encoding (Creating Digital Accessibility):**
1. Understanding and promoting digital accessibility
2. Creating accessible content
3. Using and developing accessible technologies

**Decoding (Understanding and Using Digital Accessibility):**
1. Interpreting accessible digital content
2. Obtaining and using feedback
3. Developing inclusive solutions

### Section 5: Discussion & Conclusion

**Benefits of DAL:**
1. Addresses "best practice problem"
2. Promotes interdisciplinary collaboration
3. Builds pedagogical culture

**Comparison with ARA [2]:**
- DAL: Early-stage framework, broader applicability
- ARA: Well-developed, CS-focused
- Both: Share awareness, creation, evaluation goals
- DAL strength: Complementary encoding/decoding perspectives

**Future Work:**
- Needs refinement and practice examples
- Can foster dialogue between disciplines
- Promotes extended professionalism
