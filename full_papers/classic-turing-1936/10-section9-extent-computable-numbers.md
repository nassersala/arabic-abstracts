# Section 9: The Extent of Computable Numbers

### English Version

This section argues that the class of computable numbers (in Turing's sense) coincides with what we intuitively mean by "effectively calculable."

**Arguments for completeness:**

1. **Intuitive plausibility:** Any process that a human computer could carry out using paper and pencil can be simulated by a Turing machine.

2. **Psychological argument:** The limitations of human computation (finite memory states, discrete steps, local operations) match the limitations built into Turing machines.

3. **Comparison with other formalisms:** Church's λ-calculus defines exactly the same class of computable functions, providing independent confirmation.

**Church-Turing Thesis:** The informal claim that "every effectively calculable function is Turing computable" is now called the Church-Turing Thesis. While not a mathematical theorem (since "effectively calculable" is informal), it is universally accepted.

**Implications:** If the Church-Turing Thesis is correct, then:
- Turing's negative results (undecidability of halting, Entscheidungsproblem) are absolute limitations, not artifacts of a particular formalism
- Any computational device, no matter how constructed, cannot compute anything that a Turing machine cannot
- The concept of "algorithm" has been successfully captured mathematically
