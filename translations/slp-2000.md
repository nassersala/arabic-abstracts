### English Abstract
Superword Level Parallelism (SLP) is a form of short-vector parallelism that exploits parallelism among independent isomorphic operations within basic blocks. SLP unifies loop-based and basic-block-based approaches to vectorization, enabling compilers to automatically exploit the multimedia instruction sets (SIMD extensions) available in modern processors such as Intel SSE, AMD 3DNow!, and Motorola AltiVec. The key insight is that many programs contain groups of adjacent independent scalar operations that operate on similar data types and can be packed together into short SIMD vector operations. The SLP algorithm works by identifying isomorphic groups of operations in straight-line code, building a dependence graph, packing operations into SIMD instructions, and scheduling the resulting vector code. Unlike traditional loop vectorization, SLP can exploit parallelism in unrolled loops, basic blocks without loops, and even after loop unrolling has been performed. The technique handles complex patterns including reductions, horizontal operations, and misaligned memory accesses. Experimental results on multimedia applications show speedups of 1.5-2.5x through automatic SLP optimization. The algorithm has been integrated into production compilers including GCC and LLVM, where it forms the basis for automatic vectorization of straight-line code.
